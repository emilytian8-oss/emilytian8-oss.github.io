Frequently Asked Questions

Here you can find answers to common questions about multilayer PCB, HDI PCB, high-frequency PCB, rigid-flex PCB, blind & buried via PCB, impedance-controlled PCB and hybrid-lamination PCB. For more details, please contact us at sales@yudapcb.com.

Multilayer PCB

Q1: What information do you need to quote a multilayer PCB? +

For multilayer PCB quotation, please provide your complete Gerber files and the following key parameters:

  • Layer count (for example: 4L / 6L / 8L)
  • Board thickness and copper thickness (finished)
  • Material type (standard FR‑4 or special requirements, Tg value)
  • Surface finish (HASL, ENIG, OSP, etc.)
  • Solder mask and silkscreen color
  • Quantity and expected lead time

With this information, we can evaluate manufacturability and provide the most suitable price and lead time.

HDI PCB

Q2: What HDI capability do you support and how should I design microvias? +

We support up to 3rd‑order HDI (3‑step build-up), including blind, buried and stacked microvias. When designing HDI PCBs, please:

  • Indicate the stack‑up and via types clearly in your drawing (L1‑L2 blind, L2‑L3 buried, etc.).
  • Follow our recommended minimum laser via and capture pad size for better yield.
  • Mark any via‑in‑pad areas and impedance‑controlled traces for engineering review.

Our engineering team will check your HDI design and provide DFM feedback before production.

High-Frequency PCB

Q3: How should I choose material and stackup for high-frequency PCBs? +

For high-frequency or high-speed applications, material and stackup selection is critical. We can work with FR‑4 mixed with high-frequency materials such as Rogers, etc. When sending your files, please let us know:

  • Target impedance values and tolerance
  • Operating frequency range and application type (RF, microwave, high‑speed digital)
  • Preferred material brand and dielectric constant, if you already have a specification

Based on this, we can suggest suitable materials and stackup to achieve both performance and reasonable cost.

Rigid-Flex PCB

Q4: What should I pay attention to when designing rigid-flex PCBs? +

Rigid‑flex PCB design is more complex than standard rigid boards. We recommend:

  • Clearly defining the rigid and flex areas in the drawing and Gerber files.
  • Avoiding vias and pads in dynamic bending areas of the flex section.
  • Ensuring enough copper balancing and strain relief in transition areas.
  • Providing 3D or mechanical drawings if there are special stacking or bending requirements.

Our engineers will check your files and give DFM suggestions to improve reliability and manufacturability.

Blind & Buried Via PCB

Q5: When do I need blind and buried vias and what information should I provide? +

Blind and buried vias are typically used in high‑density designs where there is not enough space for traditional through‑holes. When requesting this type of PCB, please:

  • Indicate the exact via structures in your stack‑up (for example, L1‑L2 blind, L2‑L5 buried).
  • Specify whether stacked or staggered vias are required.
  • Mark any via‑in‑pad locations and critical high‑density areas.

We will evaluate the process flow and confirm if your design is compatible with our manufacturing capabilities.

Impedance-Controlled PCB

Q6: What data do you need to control impedance on multilayer PCBs? +

For impedance-controlled PCBs, please provide:

  • An impedance table or drawing, showing target impedance values and line widths/layer locations.
  • Stack‑up diagram (layer structure), material type and dielectric thickness.
  • Any special tolerance requirement (for example, ±10% or tighter).

We will use our impedance models to calculate and adjust the line width if necessary, and impedance coupons can be measured to verify the result.

Hybrid-Lamination PCB

Q7: What is a hybrid-lamination PCB and when should I use it? +

A hybrid-lamination PCB uses different materials in one stack‑up, for example combining FR‑4 with high-frequency material or metal‑core to balance cost, performance and heat dissipation. You may consider hybrid‑lamination when:

  • Part of the circuit requires high‑frequency or low‑loss material, while the rest can use FR‑4.
  • There are strict thermal management requirements in local areas.
  • You want to optimize overall cost without sacrificing key performance.

Please provide your target performance and cost goals, and our team will help design a suitable hybrid stack‑up solution.
